1. Introduction¶

In October 2015, a data journalist named Walt Hickey analyzed movie ratings data and found strong evidence to suggest that Fandango's rating system was biased and dishonest (Fandango is an online movie ratings aggregator). He published his analysis in this article — a great piece of data journalism.

In this project, we'll analyze more recent movie ratings data to determine whether there has been any change in Fandango's rating system after Hickey's analysis.

Lets load the data:

2.1 Isolate Relevant Columns¶

We will isolate the columns that offer information about Fandango's ratings in separate variables so we have just the data we're interested in more readily available for later use.

In [4]:
old_ratings_fandango = old_ratings[['FILM', 'Fandango_Stars', 'Fandango_Ratingvalue', 'Fandango_votes', 'Fandango_Difference']]
new_ratings_fandango = new_ratings[['movie', 'year', 'fandango']]

2.2 Understanding the Population¶

Our goal is to determine whether there has been any change in Fandango's rating system after Hickey's analysis. Thus, the population of interest must be all Fandango ratings, both from before and after Hickey's analysis (Aug. 24, 2015).

Both samples are unfortunately not completely random. More specifically, the movie ratings before Aug. 24 2015 was only included if they had 30 or more votes and had tickets on sale. The movie ratings of the new_ratings dataset were only included to the dataset if the rating had a significant number of votes (not specificly defined) and the it is only ratings from movies released from year 2016 and forward.

Thus, the 2 samples are not representative for our populations of interest which would answer our question.

2.3 Alter Current Goal¶

Instead of collecting new data, we can alter our goal to fit the data we have available. We can change the goal from investigating whether Fandango's rating system has changed after Hiceky's analysis, to determine if there is any major differences in the ratings of popular movies between 2015 and 2016. The 2 samples whould be able to answer that question.

3. Isolating the Samples We Need¶

With the new goal, we now have two populations that we want to describe and compare with each other:

  • All Fandango's ratings for popular movies released in 2015.
  • All Fandango's ratings for popular movies released in 2016.

The term "popular" is vague and we need to define it with precision before continuing. We'll use Hickey's benchmark of 30 fan ratings and consider a movie as "popular" only if it has 30 fan ratings or more on Fandango's website.

Since the significantly number of votes in the ratings for the 2016 dataset was not specifically defined, we will need to ensure that at least a good portion of the ratings (e.g. above 80%) does indeed have 30 or more ratings.

We can investigate this by drawing a random sample, from the 2016 sample:

4. Comparing Distribution Shapes for 2015 and 2016¶

After all these preliminary steps, we can now start analyzing the two samples we isolated before. Once again, our goal is to determine whether there's any difference between Fandango's ratings for popular movies in 2015 and Fandango's ratings for popular movies in 2016.

There are many ways we can go about with our analysis, but let's start simple with making a high-level comparison between the shapes of the distributions of movie ratings for both samples.

In [18]:
%matplotlib inline
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from numpy import arange
plt.style.use('fivethirtyeight')
old_ratings_fandango['Fandango_Stars'].plot.kde(label='2015', legend=True, title='The Distribution of Movie Ratings by Year', figsize = (8,5.5))
new_ratings_fandango['fandango'].plot.kde(label='2016', legend=True)
plt.xlabel('Stars')
plt.xlim(0,5)
plt.xticks(arange(0,5.1,.5))
plt.legend()
plt.show()

By looking at the above density plots we can find some quite interesting observations:

  • The 2015 distribution is slightly negatively skewed, whereas the 2016 distribution has a more normalized shape.
  • The frequency distribution of movie ratings from 2016 seems to be localized on a marginally lower rating than the frequency distribution of movie ratings from 2015.
  • The two modes from each plot are approximately 0.5 rating appart.

5. Determine the Direction of the Change¶

We'll take a couple of summary statistics (remember the distinction between sample statistics and population parameters) to get a more precise picture about the direction of the difference. We'll take each distribution of movie ratings and compute its mean, median, and mode, and then compare these statistics to determine what they tell about the direction of the difference.

In [21]:
mean_2015 = old_ratings_fandango['Fandango_Stars'].mean()
mean_2016 = new_ratings_fandango['fandango'].mean()

median_2015 = old_ratings_fandango['Fandango_Stars'].median()
median_2016 = new_ratings_fandango['fandango'].median()

mode_2015 = old_ratings_fandango['Fandango_Stars'].mode()[0] # the output of Series.mode() is a bit uncommon
mode_2016 = new_ratings_fandango['fandango'].mode()[0]

summary = pd.DataFrame()
summary['2015'] = [mean_2015, median_2015, mode_2015]
summary['2016'] = [mean_2016, median_2016, mode_2016]
summary.index = ['mean', 'median', 'mode']
print(summary)

            2015      2016
mean    4.085271  3.887435
median  4.000000  4.000000
mode    4.500000  4.000000

As we also could gauge from the plot, these mean values suggests star ratings are indeed lower in the movies from 2016. While there seems to be a difference, it is worth noting that the size is very small and we do not know if it is statistical significant.

As an exstra note, the difference in mode is 0.5 which was also confirmed by the two peaks in the plot.

5.1 Grouped Bar Plot¶

Let's wrap this up by generating a grouped bar plot, which will show comparatively how the mean, median and mode varied for 2015 and 2016.

6. Conclusion¶

As we been through generating density plots, frequency distribution tables, descriptive statistics and finally grouped bar plot, we can outline these findings:

  • There seems to be a difference between the movie star ratings by movies from 2015 and 2016.
  • The difference is very small, thus it is unconfirmed if it is statistical significant

In conclusion we can answer our question by saying that there is indeed a slightly reduced movie score in movies from 2016, compared to movies from 2015, albeit very small difference.
